Alcohol Statistics: Ireland and the World

Official Irish and international statistics, source links, and Mosaic notes on alcohol use, treatment, and recovery support.

CSO Irish Health Survey 2024 — Key Findings

The Central Statistics Office Irish Health Survey 2024 reports that 81.1% of adults drank alcohol in the previous 12 months, and 40.6% drank alcohol at least weekly.

The same key findings page also reports that 75.8% of adults considered their general health to be good or very good. This is self-reported health, meaning it is based on how people describe their own health.

Mosaic note: these figures suggest that self-reported health may not always show hidden difficulties such as regular alcohol use, mental health pressure, isolation, or people delaying support. A person may describe themselves as generally healthy while still drinking regularly or struggling privately.

Mosaic is interested in that space between “I am fine” and “I need help”, where private, non-judgemental support may help someone take the next safe step.

Source: Central Statistics Office, Irish Health Survey 2024 — Key Findings.

HRB Alcohol Treatment Demand in Ireland 2024

The Health Research Board reported that 8,745 cases were treated for problem alcohol use in Ireland in 2024. This was the highest alcohol treatment figure reported in over ten years.

The HRB bulletin covers treatment cases from 2017 to 2024. It reports that the proportion of alcohol-dependent treatment cases decreased from 72% in 2017 to 56% in 2024.

The report also shows that the median age of alcohol treatment cases was 43 years old, 60% of cases were male, almost half were unemployed, and 8% were homeless.

Among people who had consumed alcohol in the 30 days before treatment, one in two drank daily during that time.

Mosaic note: these figures show that alcohol treatment demand in Ireland remains serious. They also show why support may be needed before, during, and after formal treatment. Mosaic is not a treatment service, but it aims to offer private, everyday recovery support between meetings, appointments, and difficult moments.

Source: Health Research Board, Alcohol Treatment Demand in Ireland 2024. Published 31 July 2025.

HSE Weekly Low-Risk Alcohol Guidelines

The HSE says there is no level of alcohol use that is safe for health. Its weekly low-risk guidelines are intended to reduce the risk of alcohol-related harm.

The HSE low-risk weekly guidelines say adults should not drink more than 11 standard drinks per week for women and 17 standard drinks per week for men. The HSE also says drinks should be spread out across the week.

The HSE says people should have at least 2 to 3 alcohol-free days per week, and should not drink more than 6 standard drinks on any one occasion.

In Ireland, one standard drink contains 10 grams of pure alcohol. The HSE gives examples such as half a pint of beer, stout or cider, a single measure of spirits, a small glass of wine, or an alcopop as roughly one standard drink.

Mosaic note: these guidelines give a clear Irish reference point for understanding alcohol use. They may help someone compare their own drinking with official low-risk guidance before deciding whether to cut down, ask questions, or look for support.

Source: HSE, Weekly low-risk alcohol guidelines. Page last reviewed 29 July 2025.

WHO Alcohol Deaths Worldwide

The World Health Organization reports that around 2.6 million deaths worldwide were caused by alcohol consumption in 2019.

Of these deaths, 1.6 million were from noncommunicable diseases, 700,000 were from injuries, and 300,000 were from communicable diseases.

Mosaic note: this shows that alcohol harm is not a small or private issue. It is a major worldwide health problem.

Source: World Health Organization, Alcohol fact sheet. Published 28 June 2024.

WHO Alcohol Use Disorders

The World Health Organization estimates that 400 million people aged 15 years and older lived with alcohol use disorders.

Of these, 209 million people lived with alcohol dependence.

Mosaic note: this is one of the clearest reasons for private recovery support. A very large number of people may need support, structure, encouragement, and connection.

Source: World Health Organization, Alcohol fact sheet. Published 28 June 2024.

WHO Younger People and Alcohol Harm

The World Health Organization reports that people aged 20 to 39 were disproportionately affected by alcohol consumption.

In 2019, this age group had the highest proportion of alcohol-attributable deaths, at 13%.

Mosaic note: alcohol harm is not only an older-person issue. Younger adults are also seriously affected, which supports the need for accessible, private, everyday support.

Source: World Health Organization, Alcohol fact sheet. Published 28 June 2024.

WHO Alcohol Health Risks

The World Health Organization states that alcohol consumption plays a causal role in more than 200 diseases, injuries, and other health conditions.

Alcohol is linked with liver disease, heart disease, several cancers, depression, anxiety, alcohol use disorders, and injuries.

The World Health Organization also states that alcohol is an established carcinogen.

Mosaic note: this shows that alcohol harm is wider than drinking itself. It can affect physical health, mental health, safety, family life, and daily stability.

Source: World Health Organization, Alcohol fact sheet. Published 28 June 2024.

WHO Alcohol Treatment and Awareness Gap

The World Health Organization states that access to screening, brief intervention, treatment, and medication for people with hazardous alcohol use and alcohol use disorder remains very low.

In countries where data is available, the proportion of people with alcohol use disorders in contact with treatment services ranges from less than 1% to no more than 14%.

Mosaic note: this is a key reason for Mosaic. Low access is not only about services being unavailable. Many people may not know what help exists, where to start, who to contact, or what kind of support is suitable for them.

If someone breaks a leg, they usually know they need a hospital. But someone can struggle with alcohol for years without knowing that treatment centres, recovery meetings, support services, and practical help are out there. Mosaic aims to make support easier to find, easier to understand, and easier to reach.

Source: World Health Organization, Alcohol fact sheet. Published 28 June 2024.
