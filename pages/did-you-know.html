Did You Know?

Addiction and recovery facts from trusted sources.

01 Did You Know?

Cravings pass. Cravings can feel overwhelming in the moment, but they do not last forever. Many people in recovery describe cravings as waves that build, peak, and then gradually fade away. Learning to delay a reaction, distract yourself, or reach out for support can help you ride out the urge without acting on it.

Courtesy of HSE Addiction Services Learn More Back to top

02 Did You Know?

Recovery is rarely a straight line. Many people experience setbacks, periods of stagnation, and even relapse before moving forward again. Recovery is often described as a lifelong journey rather than a destination, requiring ongoing effort, self-awareness, and growth.

Recovery also affects more than substance use alone. It can improve physical health, relationships, confidence, finances, and overall wellbeing. Because every person's circumstances are different, no two recovery journeys look exactly the same.

Courtesy of Never Alone Recovery Learn More Back to top

03 Did You Know?

Many young people overestimate how much alcohol or cannabis their peers use. Research suggests that correcting these misconceptions can influence attitudes towards substance use and reduce risky behaviours, including driving under the influence or riding with an impaired driver.

Researchers found that a short, non-judgemental educational intervention delivered alongside driver education could help challenge beliefs about substance use and encourage safer decision-making. The approach focused on personal choice, accurate information, and practical strategies for staying safe.

Courtesy of Recovery Research Institute Learn More Back to top

04 Did You Know?

Many people initially use drugs because they produce pleasurable feelings. However, repeated use can change how the brain responds to rewards. Over time, activities that were once enjoyable may become less satisfying, while the urge to use the drug becomes stronger. In some cases, people find themselves using the drug simply to feel normal rather than to feel good.

Courtesy of National Institute on Drug Abuse (NIDA) Learn More Back to top

05 Did You Know?

Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorders (FASD) is a term used to describe a range of physical, behavioural, and cognitive difficulties that can occur when a baby is exposed to alcohol before birth. The effects can vary from person to person and may not become obvious until later in childhood, but they can last throughout life.

Research shows that alcohol can interfere with the development of a baby's brain and other organs at any stage of pregnancy, including before a person realises they are pregnant. While binge drinking and heavy drinking increase the risk of severe harm, researchers have not identified a safe amount of alcohol that can be consumed during pregnancy without risk.

Courtesy of National Institute on Alcohol Abuse and Alcoholism (NIAAA) Learn More Back to top

06 Did You Know?

Alcohol dependence and alcohol abuse are not the same thing. Alcohol dependence occurs when a person continues drinking to avoid withdrawal symptoms such as tremors, insomnia, mood changes, or cravings. Alcohol abuse, on the other hand, refers to continued excessive drinking despite negative consequences such as health problems, relationship difficulties, legal issues, or problems at work.

Modern medical guidelines now group both conditions under the term "Alcohol Use Disorder" (AUD), which exists on a spectrum ranging from mild to severe. This change reflects the understanding that alcohol problems can be serious even when physical dependence is not present.

Courtesy of Verywell Mind Learn More Back to top

07 Did You Know?

Support matters. Recovery does not have to be a journey taken alone. Whether support comes from family, friends, recovery groups, counsellors, or treatment services, having people to talk to can make difficult periods easier to manage. Seeking support is a sign of strength, not weakness.

Courtesy of Drugs.ie Learn More Back to top

08 Did You Know?

People do not choose to become addicted. Research shows that addiction is influenced by a combination of biological, environmental, and life experience factors. Genetics, stress, early exposure to substances, isolation, trauma, and social influences can all affect a person's risk of developing addiction.

This helps explain why addiction cannot simply be overcome through willpower alone. Like many other health conditions, addiction develops through a complex interaction of risk factors that differ from person to person.

Courtesy of Alcohol and Drug Foundation (Australia) Learn More Back to top

09 Did You Know?

Supervised Consumption Sites are often discussed as a way to prevent overdose deaths, but they also provide access to healthcare, social services, wound care, mental health support, and addiction treatment referrals. Many people who use these services would otherwise have little contact with healthcare professionals.

According to Canadian data, there were more than 60,000 overdose events recorded at supervised consumption sites between 2017 and 2024, with no onsite fatalities reported. Researchers also found that these sites can reduce public drug use and help connect people to additional support services.

Courtesy of the Canadian Centre on Substance Use and Addiction (CCSA) Learn More Back to top

10 Did You Know?

Many people are aware of the link between smoking and cancer, but fewer realise that alcohol is also a recognised cause of cancer. Research shows that drinking alcohol increases the risk of cancers of the mouth, throat, voice box, oesophagus, colon and rectum, liver, and breast.

According to the CDC, all alcoholic drinks increase cancer risk, including beer, wine, and spirits. Drinking less alcohol reduces this risk.

Courtesy of the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) Learn More Back to top

11 Did You Know?

An alcohol blackout is not the same as passing out. During a blackout, a person can remain awake, hold conversations, and continue interacting with others, but their brain is temporarily unable to form new memories.

Blackouts occur when alcohol interferes with the brain's ability to transfer information into long-term memory. In severe cases, entire hours of events may be permanently lost, even though the person appeared conscious at the time.

Courtesy of the National Institute on Alcohol Abuse and Alcoholism (NIAAA) Learn More Back to top

12 Did You Know?

Alcohol does more than affect judgement and memory. Research suggests that heavy alcohol exposure can disrupt the function of mitochondria — the tiny structures inside cells that produce the energy needed for brain cells to communicate and form memories.

Scientists believe this disruption may contribute to learning difficulties, memory problems, inflammation, and other cognitive changes associated with excessive alcohol use.

Courtesy of Drug and Alcohol Dependence Journal Learn More Back to top

13 Did You Know?

Your gut and brain are constantly communicating with each other. Most people think stress affects the stomach, but the connection works both ways. Signals from the gut can also influence mood, anxiety, and emotional wellbeing.

Researchers have found that a troubled gut can send distress signals to the brain, while stress, anxiety, and depression can affect how the digestive system functions. This two-way communication is often called the "gut-brain connection."

Courtesy of Harvard Health Publishing Learn More Back to top

14 Did You Know?

Research suggests that stigma can be a barrier to recovery. People with substance use disorders often face negative stereotypes and discrimination, which can discourage them from seeking treatment or support.

Studies have also shown that language matters. Terms such as "addict" or "abuser" can reinforce stigma, while person-first language such as "person with a substance use disorder" can help reduce negative attitudes and encourage people to seek help.

Courtesy of the National Institute on Drug Abuse (NIDA) Learn More Back to top

15 Did You Know?

Many people believe alcohol improves sleep because it can make them feel relaxed and help them fall asleep more quickly. However, researchers have found that alcohol can interfere with the normal sleep cycle, causing sleep to become lighter and more fragmented as the night progresses.

Alcohol can also reduce REM sleep, the stage of sleep associated with memory formation, learning, and emotional processing. This means a person may spend enough time in bed but still wake feeling tired, less focused, and less refreshed than they would after a natural night's sleep.

Courtesy of Sleep Foundation Learn More Back to top
