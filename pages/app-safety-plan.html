Safety Plan

A safety plan is a plan you make while you are calm, so you know what to do when things do not feel calm. It is for moments when things feel unsafe, risky, or too much to handle alone.

Visit Main Website

What is a safety plan?

A safety plan is a next-step plan

It is not about fixing everything at once. It helps you know what to do next if you might drink, use, gamble, harm yourself, disappear, isolate, go somewhere unsafe, contact the wrong person, or feel unable to cope.

It is made before things get worse

The best time to make a safety plan is before a crisis. When pressure is high, it can be hard to think clearly. A written plan gives you something simple to follow when your head is busy.

It should be simple

A safety plan should be clear enough to use on a bad day. Short steps are better than perfect words. The aim is safety, support, and getting through the next part without making things worse.

When should I use my safety plan?

Use your safety plan early. You do not have to wait until everything is urgent.

Use it when What it may mean
I feel close to drinking, using, or gambling. I need to move away from risk and contact support.
I am thinking about harming myself or someone else. I need urgent support now.
I feel unable to cope. I need to reduce pressure and not stay alone with it.
I am isolating, hiding, lying, or disappearing. I may be drifting away from recovery and need connection.
I am going near risky people, places, or situations. I need to change direction before things build up.

Step 1: Notice the warning signs

Warning signs are signals. They do not mean you have failed. They mean it is time to use support earlier.

Thought warning signs

“I do not care anymore.” “I can handle one.” “Nobody would miss me.” “I’ll just disappear.” “There is no point.” “I deserve this.”

Feeling warning signs

Anger, panic, shame, loneliness, boredom, numbness, hopelessness, overwhelm, resentment, fear, or feeling trapped.

Behaviour warning signs

Isolating, cancelling plans, hiding how you feel, going near risky places, contacting unsafe people, skipping support, not eating, not sleeping, or stopping healthy routines.

Step 2: Make the situation safer now

Move away from risk

Leave the pub, off-licence, argument, unsafe house, risky person, gambling site, or anything else that is pulling you towards danger.

Do not stay alone if alone feels unsafe

Go somewhere safer if you can. This could be a public place, a recovery space, a waiting room, a coffee shop, a support service, or near someone steady.

Put distance between you and anything dangerous

If something could be used to harm yourself or someone else, move away from it or ask someone safe to help you make the area safer.

Delay the risky decision

You do not have to solve everything. Delay the action. Wait ten minutes. Open the app. Contact support. Let the strongest part of the urge pass.

Step 3: Get through the next 10 minutes

In a difficult moment, ten minutes can matter. Pick one small action.

Small action Why it may help
Open Hold the Line. Use the app before the pressure gets bigger.
Drink water or eat something. Bring the body down first if the mind is racing.
Write one honest sentence. Get the truth out of your head and onto the page.
Step outside or change rooms. A physical change can interrupt a risky pattern.
Send one short message. You do not have to explain everything to ask for support.

Step 4: Contact support

Support does not have to mean family or friends. It can be one safe person, a professional, a helpline, a recovery contact, or a service.

A simple message

“I do not feel safe with my own thoughts right now. Can you stay in contact with me for a while?”

“I am close to doing something risky and I need help to get through the next part.”

“I am trying not to drink/use/gamble today. Can I talk to you for a few minutes?”

“I need support now. I do not need you to fix it. I just need not to be alone with it.”

If no personal contact is available

Use a professional service, helpline, GP out-of-hours, emergency department, or emergency services if needed. A support network can include services as well as people.

My simple safety plan

Use these prompts in your Mosaic journal, on paper, or with someone you trust. Leave the answers blank until you are making your own plan.

Question My answer
How do I know I am becoming unsafe?  
What do I need to move away from?  
Where can I go that is safer?  
What can I do for the next 10 minutes?  
Who or what can I contact?  
What service can I use if no one answers?  
When do I need emergency help?  

If there is immediate danger

Emergency help

If you or someone else is about to be harmed, call emergency services on 112 or 999, or go to the nearest emergency department.

Do not wait alone

If you feel unable to stay safe, contact urgent support now. Move away from anything dangerous if possible and get near help.

Review the plan when things are calmer

A safety plan can change. After a difficult moment, look back gently and ask what helped, what did not help, and what needs to be clearer next time.

Review question My answer
What warning sign did I notice?  
What helped me stay safer?  
What made things harder?  
What should I add to my plan?  
Who or what support do I need next?
