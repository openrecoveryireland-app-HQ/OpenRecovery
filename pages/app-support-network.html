Support Network

Not everyone has family or friends they can safely turn to. Support can come from many places: one trusted person, someone in recovery, a counsellor, a GP, a support worker, an aftercare service, a helpline, or a local community service. This page is here to help you think about safe support, not perfect support.

Visit Main Website

Support does not have to mean family

If I have safe family or friends

Some people do have family or friends who are safe, steady, and helpful. They may not understand everything, but they may still be able to listen, check in, help with practical things, or support recovery in small ways.

If I do not have safe family or friends

That does not mean you have no support. A support network can be built from professionals, recovery contacts, helplines, community services, peer support, aftercare, online support, or one person who is safe enough to contact today.

If I feel alone

Feeling alone can make everything heavier. The next step does not have to be big. It might be opening the app, writing one honest sentence, sending one message, using a helpline, or looking up one service.

If people around me are not safe

Support should not make you feel threatened, controlled, judged, pressured, or pushed towards old behaviour. It is okay to protect your recovery and keep distance from people or places that put you at risk.

What makes someone safe to contact?

A safe person does not have to be perfect. Look for someone who helps you move towards recovery, honesty, calm, and support.

Safe support may look like Be careful if it looks like
They listen without mocking or shaming you. They make you feel small, guilty, stupid, or weak.
They respect your recovery and your boundaries. They pressure you towards alcohol, drugs, gambling, conflict, or chaos.
They are steady enough to contact when you are struggling. They make the situation more dramatic, risky, or unsafe.
They encourage honest next steps. They encourage secrecy, excuses, lying, or hiding.
They help you feel more grounded. They leave you feeling more anxious, angry, ashamed, or alone.

What kind of support do I need?

I need someone to listen

Sometimes you do not need advice straight away. You may just need to say what is happening out loud, without pretending you are fine.

I need help staying safe in recovery

If you are close to a risky situation, contact support early. You do not need to wait until everything is urgent. Use Mosaic, your saved supports, or a professional service before the pressure gets stronger.

I need practical support

Practical problems can affect recovery. Money, housing, forms, appointments, work, study, transport, or daily routine may need practical help rather than emotional advice.

Go to Practical Help

I need professional support

A professional support can be part of your network. This might be a GP, counsellor, addiction service, aftercare worker, mental health service, community worker, or helpline.

How to ask for support

You do not have to explain everything perfectly. A short, honest message can be enough.

Simple messages

“I’m having a hard day and I do not want to isolate.”

“Can I talk to you for a few minutes?”

“I’m trying to stay on track today. Could you check in with me later?”

“I do not need you to fix it. I just need to say it out loud.”

“I’m not feeling steady and I need to use support before it gets worse.”

If you do not know what to say

Keep it short. Say what is true, say what you need, and send it before you talk yourself out of asking.

Support network check-in

Use these prompts in your Mosaic journal, on paper, or with someone you trust.

Question My answer
Who feels safe enough to contact today?  
What kind of support do I need?  
Who or what should I avoid today?  
What message could I send?  
What service could help if no person is available?  

Building support slowly

Start with one safe contact

You do not need a big circle of people. One steady contact is better than ten unsafe ones.

Use services as part of the network

Support does not have to come only from personal relationships. A helpline, GP, counsellor, addiction service, aftercare group, or local community service can be part of your support network.

Protect your boundaries

You are allowed to choose who gets access to you. You do not have to explain your recovery to people who make it harder.

Keep coming back

Connection can take time to rebuild. A small step today still counts, even if it is only opening the app, writing honestly, or finding one service.

Irish support options

HSE Drugs and Alcohol Helpline

The HSE Drugs and Alcohol Helpline offers confidential information, support, guidance and referral for drug and alcohol concerns in Ireland.

Freephone: 1800 459 459
Email: helpline@hse.ie
Monday to Friday: 9.30am to 5.30pm

Visit Website

Drugs.ie National Directory

The Drugs.ie directory can help you find drug and alcohol services and specialist treatment programmes in Ireland, including support, counselling, education, training, and treatment services.

Visit Website

HSE Your Mental Health information line

This is a 24/7 information line for mental health supports and services in Ireland. It is not a counselling service, but it can help you find out what supports are available and how to access them.

Freephone: 1800 111 888

Visit Website

Samaritans Ireland

Samaritans offers emotional support if you are struggling to cope and need someone to talk to.

Freephone: 116 123
Email: jo@samaritans.ie

Visit Website

When support feels urgent

If you feel unsafe, at risk, or unable to cope, use your Safety Plan and contact urgent support. Do not wait until things get worse.
